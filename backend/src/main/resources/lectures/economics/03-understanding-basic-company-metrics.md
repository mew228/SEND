# Company Metrics Are About The Business

In the last lessons, you worked with market values like price.

Price answers a market question:

**What are people paying for this stock right now?**

Company metrics answer a different question:

**What is the business producing underneath the stock?**

This matters because price can move for many reasons, but company metrics are meant to describe business performance. They are “fundamentals.” They typically come from a company’s financial statements, which are published on a regular schedule (often quarterly and annually), not every minute like price.

In other words:

- price is a market snapshot
- fundamentals are a business report

In a graph system, that distinction is useful. You can fetch market data every day, but you may treat company metrics as slower-moving inputs that help you decide *which* stocks your rules apply to.

A simple example is filtering:

**Only allow a buy path if the company is profitable.**

That is a fundamentals-based rule. And it starts with a few beginner metrics.

:::sublecture { "id":"revenue-is-the-top-line", "title":"Revenue Is The Top Line" }
# Revenue Is The Top Line

Revenue is the total amount of money generated by selling goods or services.

A simple way to think about it is:

**Price per unit × number of units sold**

Revenue is important because it is the beginning of the company’s story. It tells you how much business activity the company produced over a period of time.

But revenue is not the same as profit.

A company can have high revenue and still have low profit if its costs are high.

So the first beginner habit is this:

**Use revenue to understand scale, not profitability.**

In graphs, revenue can become an input that helps you set category rules.

For example:

- “Only consider companies with revenue above X.”
- “Only consider companies with revenue above X *and* price above Y.”

You do not need complex valuation to start. You just need to recognize what revenue represents.

:::sublecture { "id":"profit-and-earnings-are-whats-left", "title":"Profit And Earnings Are What’s Left" }
# Profit And Earnings Are What’s Left

If revenue is money coming in, profit is what remains after costs.

At the beginner level, profit can be thought of simply as:

**Revenue − Costs**

But in real financial statements, you will see multiple layers of “profit,” because costs come in different categories.

A helpful beginner way to read it is:

- **gross profit**: what is left after the direct costs of producing what was sold
- **operating profit**: what is left after running the business (overhead)
- **net income** (often called “earnings”): what is left after *all* expenses, including things like interest and taxes

You do not need to memorize every line item yet. You only need to understand the main idea:

**Net income is the bottom-line profit number for the period.**

That one number answers a simple question:

**Did the company make money in this period?**

And that question is extremely easy to turn into a graph rule.

For example:

- “Only allow buys when net income is above 0.”
- “Avoid buying when net income is negative.”

:::sublecture { "id":"eps-and-pe-turn-business-results-into-per-share-metrics", "title":"EPS And P/E Turn Business Results Into Per-Share Metrics" }
# EPS And P/E Turn Business Results Into Per-Share Metrics

Once you understand net income, the next step is understanding what that income means *per share*.

## Earnings per share

**Earnings per share (EPS)** is net profit divided by the number of common shares.

The reason EPS matters is that two companies might both earn $1 billion, but if one company has many more shares, then each share represents a smaller slice of that profit.

EPS helps you compare earnings in a “per-share” way.

## Price-to-earnings ratio

The **price-to-earnings ratio (P/E)** connects the stock price back to earnings.

P/E is calculated by dividing:

**stock price ÷ earnings per share**

At a beginner level, you can think of P/E like this:

**How much is the market paying for $1 of earnings?**

That does not mean a lower P/E is always “better,” or a higher P/E is always “bad.” It is a context number. It is most useful when you compare companies in similar industries and similar situations.

One important limitation to remember:

If earnings are negative, P/E may not be meaningful, and some systems will not report it.

That is why a basic fundamentals rule often starts with profitability first (earnings above 0), then uses P/E after.

:::sublecture { "id":"how-company-metrics-become-graph-rules", "title":"How Company Metrics Become Graph Rules" }
# How Company Metrics Become Graph Rules

A fundamentals rule is usually a filter.

It does not have to be your full strategy. It can simply decide whether a stock is even allowed to enter the strategy’s “decision zone.”

This is the graph pattern you already know:

1. fetch a value  
2. compare it to a rule  
3. use the result as a signal  

This time, the value is a company metric instead of a market price.

## A first fundamentals filter

Here is a beginner-friendly filter:

**Only allow the strategy to proceed if EPS is greater than 0.**

That rule is intentionally simple. It is not trying to find the perfect stock. It is only trying to avoid one obvious case: buying a company that is currently losing money on a per-share basis.

Later, you can add more conditions:

- EPS above 0 **and** P/E below a threshold
- revenue above X **and** price above Y
- earnings growing **and** price trend positive

But first, learn the shape.
